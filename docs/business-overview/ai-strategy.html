The CXPro AI Strategy

This page is the shared map between business and engineering: every AI capability we ship or plan, the hard problem it solves, and the dollar-shaped value it creates. Statuses are honest — Live ships today, Building is in active development, Next is designed and sequenced, Frontier is a researched bet that needs new data or partnerships.

North star

CXPro is the central nervous system of the project

A commissioning app cannot win in a vacuum. The facilities we serve are designed by one set of companies, built by another, powered by a regional grid, filled with vendor equipment, and handed to an operator who lives with it for 20 years. Our AI's defining advantage is that it sits between all of them — pulling design intent downstream from the people who created it, and pushing verified operational readiness upstream to the people who depend on it.

Pillar 1 — the foundation

Integration fabric: Procore, BIM, and open AI access

Procore and BIM integration is not a nice-to-have — it's the substrate every advanced AI feature feeds on. Procore tells the AI what the project decided; the BIM model tells it where everything physically is. Our own MCP interface lets anyone else's AI plug into us, which makes CXPro the easiest commissioning tool in the market to integrate with.

Procore as a live signal Next

Hard task: teams discover mid-test that a chiller model was swapped by a change order three weeks ago.
AI capability: a background agent watches Procore RFIs, submittals, and change orders; an approved equipment change automatically flags and re-drafts the affected test procedures.
Value: zero tests executed against stale designs — the most expensive class of rework on a fast-moving Texas hyperscale job.
Verified path: Procore's developer platform is fully self-service — we can build against the API and sandbox today, no partnership required. The RFI API supports the full workflow read/write. Marketplace listing (their certified partner program) comes later, as a distribution channel.

BIM / digital twin awareness Year 2

Hard task: procedures don't know which pipe feeds which valve, or which conduit powers which rack.
AI capability: the AI reads BIM geometry and metadata (voltages, flow rates, tolerances) so generated checklists are tailored to the exact approved model, and spatial relationships become queryable facts.
Value: procedure generation moves from "drafted from the spec PDF" to "drafted from the engineered truth" — fewer review edits, higher trust.
Verified path: Autodesk's AEC Data Model API exposes granular Revit data via cloud API (no plugins), and their Issues API supports commissioning as a first-class issue type. One caveat: access is gated on the customer's Autodesk entitlements.

Open AI access via MCP Building

Hard task: every partner (owner's analysts, GC's tooling, operator's AI) wants programmatic access without a custom integration project.
AI capability: CXPro exposes a curated, permission-scoped tool surface over the open MCP standard — external agents can query deviations, project health, and design intent, and draft (never commit) records. The same architecture already powers our internal agents.
Value: "integrates with anyone upstream or downstream" becomes literal — partners connect their AI to ours in hours, not quarters.
Market check: MCP is now table stakes — BlueRithm shipped one in May 2026 and Procore exposes MCP in its Agentic APIs. Our edge must be the quality of the surface: per-role permission scoping and draft-not-commit safety, which nobody pairs with it yet.

Automated closeout cleardown Frontier

Hard task: hundreds of open punch items and RFIs gate legal opening, each closed by hand.
AI capability: as tests pass, the AI verifies telemetry against success criteria, then updates Procore — closing punch items and RFIs with sensor logs attached as proof.
Value: hundreds of engineering hours recovered at exactly the moment the project is most schedule-critical.

Pillar 2 — the daily superpower

The Commissioning Copilot: answering the hardest questions on site

The most valuable AI feature isn't exotic — it's the one a CM or tech reaches for twenty times a day. CXPro's copilot is built to answer the questions that currently take hours of document archaeology, phone calls, and tribal memory — with citations, every time.

The question they actually askWhat the AI draws onStatus

Why we can answer harder questions than a chatbot: every conversation is auto-scoped to the asset, test, or deviation it was opened from, and the copilot's grounding pool is the union of documents, structured records, test history, and (as integrations land) BIM and telemetry. Hard questions are hard because the answer spans sources — our architecture is built to span them.

Pillar 3 — capture everything, lose nothing

Multimodal field capture & the lesson engine

Commissioning knowledge dies in two places: the noisy plant floor where nobody types, and the project closeout where hard-won lessons leave with the team. This pillar attacks both.

🎙️

Voice & audio Next

Hands-free voice-to-step entry for technicians in PPE on noisy floors — speak the reading, the AI structures it into the test record. Audio notes attach to steps and are transcribed, indexed, and searchable like any document.

Market proof: OpenSpace's AI Voice Notes (2025) already turns hands-free speech into structured fields and reported an 86% speed-up in issue documentation at one customer2 — the modality is proven; applying it to test execution is ours to own.

📹

Photo & video evidence Building

Photo capture per test step ships in the field experience today; video extends it — record a startup sequence or a witness demonstration, and the AI indexes it, links it to the test record, and includes it in the handover dossier as verifiable evidence.

Market proof: video→structured-data is emerging now (Track3D converts field video into production data via Procore's Agentic APIs). Witness-evidence video understanding is still unowned.

📚

Lesson logging Next

Every resolved deviation, every AI correction, every "we'll know better next time" gets captured as a structured lesson — tagged to equipment type, system, and failure mode. The AI surfaces relevant lessons proactively: start a test on a pump model that burned you last quarter, and the lesson is already on screen.

Market check: CxPlanner ships pattern analytics over test results — adjacent, but a proactive lessons-learned engine tied to procedure generation remains unowned.

The equipment logbook: where multimodal capture and lessons compound

Here's how the pieces above snap together into one simple idea: every piece of equipment accumulates a permanent multimodal record, and AI analytics runs on top of it.

Capture lands on the asset, automatically

A photo of the nameplate, a voice note during start-up ("hearing a slight rattle at 60% load"), a video of the witness demonstration, a thermal image during load testing — whoever captures it, from whatever test step, it files itself onto that asset's timeline. No uploading to the right folder, no naming conventions. AHU-42's record builds itself while the team just works.

One logbook per asset, searchable like a conversation

Open any unit and its full commissioning life is there: every test, result, deviation, photo, transcribed voice note, and video — in order. Ask the AI "show me everything we captured on this chiller during L3" or "what did the tech say about the rattle?" and get answers with the original media one click away.

AI analytics at three altitudes

This unit: compare today's readings, sounds, and thermal images against the unit's own baseline — "vibration at L4 is up versus its L3 start-up." This model, fleet-wide: patterns across identical equipment — "3 of the 12 units of this pump model logged the same start-up noise; the other 9 didn't — inspect before IST." This program: the lesson engine mines all of it, so your next project starts knowing what this one learned the hard way. Lessons are scoped to your organization — your accumulated knowledge is never visible to anyone else.

The logbook ships with the building

At handover the operator doesn't get a binder — they get every unit's living history: what it sounded like healthy, what its thermal profile was at acceptance, every quirk the field team ever noted. Day-2 operations and future warranty claims start from evidence, not memory.

Why this is the compounding moat: each capture is cheap for the person making it and permanent for everyone after them. The fleet-wide signature dataset this builds is exactly what the acoustic/thermal early-failure bet (Pillar 4) needs — and it can't be bought or scraped; it has to be accumulated project by project. Whoever owns the equipment logbook owns the data the next decade of commissioning AI will be built on.

Pillar 4 — the frontier bets

Four hard problems worth winning

These are the capabilities that separate "useful tool" from "category-defining platform." Each targets a problem today's market leaders don't solve. They are staged deliberately — every one depends on the foundation pillars above, and each is honest about what it needs to become real.

Sequence-of-operations stress testing Frontier

Hard task: finding subtle control-logic flaws across thousands of BMS points before live testing.
AI capability: the AI converts written sequences and drawings into a simulation model and runs massive automated scenario sweeps against the vendor's control logic — surfacing failures like "if Chiller 2 fails at exactly 38.5°C outdoor air, valve V-102 reacts too slowly, causing a thermal spike."
Value: logic flaws caught in simulation cost minutes; the same flaw caught during an integrated systems test costs days of a fully-staffed site.
Depends on: BIM integration, extracted sequences (live today), vendor control-code access partnerships.

Unified trip chronology Frontier

Hard task: when a breaker trips during an IST, the power monitoring system, the BMS, and individual devices all log it at slightly different timestamps — root-cause analysis becomes guesswork.
AI capability: the AI realigns mismatched device logs into a single microsecond-accurate playback of the event, so the team sees the true failure path instead of arguing about it.
Value: root-cause sessions collapse from days of log forensics across three vendors to a single visual replay.
Depends on: EPMS/BMS telemetry ingestion (rail #5 above).
White space check: confirmed unowned — EPMS systems already log millisecond power-quality data, but the most advanced shipping "analytics" (Niagara) are rule-based, not AI. Nobody reconstructs unified chronologies.

Acoustic & thermal early-failure detection Frontier

Hard task: equipment that passes its load bank test but is quietly flawed and fails in month 18 — inside the warranty dispute zone.
AI capability: during L4 testing, mobile devices or site sensors capture vibration signatures, thermal images, and acoustic profiles; the AI compares them against known-failure patterns and flags units that pass today but won't last — "this generator's acoustic signature indicates a misaligned shaft bearing."
Value: a failure caught at commissioning is a vendor warranty claim; the same failure at month 18 is an operational outage. This is also where our audio/video capture pillar pays its second dividend — the evidence is already flowing through the platform.
Depends on: multimodal capture (Pillar 3) and an accumulated signature dataset (the lesson engine).
White space check: unowned at the commissioning phase — the underlying ML (vibration/acoustic analysis of rotating machinery) has a decade of peer-reviewed maturity, but it's deployed for operations monitoring, not acceptance testing. The honest risk: little labeled failure data exists for brand-new equipment, which is exactly why our lesson engine must feed this bet.

Crew readiness analytics Frontier

Hard task: the facility passed every test — but can the night crew actually handle a real grid failure?
AI capability: during L5 blackout simulations, the AI observes operational response — time-to-procedure, alarm handling, action sequence — and scores human readiness with targeted findings: "backup power came up, but the crew skipped isolating the secondary loop; a 22+ minute outage would have lost cooling."
Value: turns the soft, unmeasured half of operational readiness into a scored, trainable deliverable owners will pay for — and a differentiated line item in every handover package.
Depends on: IST execution data (live today) plus structured crisis-scenario instrumentation.
White space check: confirmed unowned — no commissioning or construction vendor touches operational readiness scoring.

Regional edge

Built for the markets we're targeting

The two launch markets demand different things from the AI — and that difference is a moat against one-size-fits-all competitors.

🤠

Texas — speed under grid stress

The market: hyperscale builds for Meta, Microsoft, and AWS, delivered at breakneck pace by GCs like DPR, Holder, and Turner (DPR delivered a 980,000 sq ft, 200 MW campus in under 18 months5), on an ERCOT grid known for instability.
The tooling reality: Procore-centric project management with CxAlloy or Excel holding the commissioning layer — our integration wedge.
What the AI must do: track design changes in real time (Procore signal) so testing never trails the build, and simulate fast-frequency response and power-shedding scenarios so generators are proven against ERCOT micro-grid events — not just nameplate specs.

🍁

Canada — precision and sovereignty

The market: sovereign-cloud and HPC campuses (Google Québec, AWS Calgary, eStruxture, QScale) with harsh winters, strict environmental rules, and data-residency requirements.
The tooling reality: the same Procore/Autodesk backbone as the US — no Canada-specific commissioning tool exists — but with heavier BIM coordination, which makes the Autodesk integration a bigger lever here.5
What the AI must do: model free-cooling glycol loops and the changeover between free and mechanical cooling against climate forecasts, handle district-heating handoffs, and keep all project data within Canadian jurisdiction.
