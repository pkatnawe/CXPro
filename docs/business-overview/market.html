Is there real value here?

The honest verdict — yes, and the market is now proving it for us

Stepping back from the feature talk: is this a real business, or a nicely-designed hobby? The June 2026 evidence says real — and three independent signals now confirm it that weren't visible a month ago.

🤝

Strategics are buying in

Accenture acquired a majority stake in DLB Associates (Dec 2025) — a firm doing data-center commissioning, construction quality, and energy optimization — explicitly to build "end-to-end data center development."3 When a global consultancy pays up for data-center commissioning capability, the value thesis isn't ours to prove anymore — the market priced it.

💸

Venture money is adjacent

Structured AI (YC, Sequoia Scout, 20VC — ~$5M) is shipping AI agents for construction QA/QC on drawings.3 It validates the thesis (AI agents for construction quality is fundable) and warns us: smart money is circling the edges of our space. We're early, not alone.

📈

The category is underpriced

From the pricing analysis below: incumbents charge ~0.01% of the commissioning budget they serve. A correctly-priced AI platform (per-MW, value-anchored) captures 100× that while staying a rounding error to the buyer. Real margin lives in that gap.

The verdict, stated honestly: the value is real and now externally validated — but the window is the asset, not the idea. Structured AI is funded, Procore and Autodesk are buying AI capability (DataGrid, Payapps, Eyrus), and ValGenesis already owns AI validation in pharma. None of them own AI-native data-center commissioning yet. The bet is winnable on speed and wedge discipline, not on the vision — and it carries a credible exit: a platform with real Cx traction is an obvious acquisition target for Procore or Autodesk (both weak on handover) or a services consolidator (Accenture just showed the appetite).

We're not just data centers

The wedge-and-expand plan: one engine, many regulated facilities

Commissioning is the same discipline everywhere — verify built matches designed, test by levels, document, hand over. Only the compliance framework changes. That makes data centers a beachhead, not the whole market. Our engine generalizes; our moat (the trust layer) matters more as the regulatory bar rises.

Pricing — fewer customers, more money

Price to a fraction of the commissioning budget — by the megawatt, locked at signing

The instinct — take a slice of the commissioning spend rather than sell cheap seats — is the right economic model, and the real numbers make it overwhelming. The refinement: charge by a clean unit that scales with project value (the megawatt), and tell the value story as a fraction of the Cx budget.

The core insight: the incumbents anchored this category absurdly low — they sell a few thousand dollars of software against a $20M+ commissioning line. We must not fight on their per-seat / per-project axis; that's a race to a tiny number. We price against the commissioning budget and the cost of delay, on a unit (the megawatt) that scales with both. The market is moving our way: ~85% of SaaS now uses some usage-based pricing, Procore itself bills by project volume not seats, and Gartner projects 40% of enterprise SaaS will be outcome-based by end of 2026.2

Why not per-seat

Per-seat punishes the thing we want — getting every sub and tech into the system. It also commoditizes us against CxAlloy's pricing. Our committed posture stays: unlimited users.

Why not a literal %

"Give us 3% of your commissioning cost" is value-aligned but procurement-hostile: it demands they open their books, it's unpredictable to meter, and buyers resist a claim on their fee. Great narrative, bad invoice.

Why per-MW wins

Data centers are sized in megawatts — it's the unit buyers already think in. Price per commissioned MW and revenue scales with project size exactly like a percentage would (a 200 MW campus pays far more than a 5 MW site), but the number is clean, known up front, and locked at signing — never metered. A single large campus is a large contract from one logo — fewer customers, more money.

The model, decided: a fixed annual platform fee per org (unlimited users + the lesson engine) + a per-MW rate locked at signing, landing around $2.5–5k/MW for standard facilities, ×1.5 for AI/liquid-cooled — roughly 1–2% of the ~$200–360k/MW commissioning budget. A 100 MW campus is ~$425k; a gigawatt buildout, ~$2.5M, from one logo. Critically, it's priced on nameplate capacity fixed in the design basis, so the buyer knows their bottom-dollar number before signing. We do not meter usage, charge per seat, or tie price to on-time handover — that depends on schedule slips the buyer doesn't control. Predictability is the product.
