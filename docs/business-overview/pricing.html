Internal · pricing model · predictable, scales with the build

One number, known before you sign — and it scales with the megawatt, not with anything you can't control.

The commissioning team already lives with enough uncertainty: slipping schedules, change orders, scarce labor. The software bill should be the one line they can budget to the dollar on day one. This is how we price — value-anchored, fully predictable, and built to scale across one campus or a whole portfolio. For the competitive and value case it sits inside, see Market & Strategy. The two non-negotiables

Predictable to the dollar. Scales with the value at stake.

Every decision on this page is forced by two rules. If a pricing idea violates either, it's out — no matter how clever the revenue story.

🔒

Predictable — bottom dollar up front

The buyer must be able to compute their total before signing and put it in the budget. The number on the quote is the number on every invoice. No metering surprises, no overage true-ups, no "it depends." A finance approver should be able to forecast the spend without a phone call — the predictability enterprise buyers explicitly require to clear a budget holder.4

🎛️

Never priced on what they can't control

We refuse to bill on the variables the buyer doesn't govern: schedule delays (driven by upstream phases), the number of failures or deviations, how many people log in, or anything that floats. Those are their risks to manage — charging on them is both unfair and impossible to budget. We price on one fixed, knowable input instead.

📈

Scales with project value

A 5 MW edge site and a gigawatt campus should not pay the same. Price rides a unit that grows with the value at stake — the megawatt — so a bigger build pays more, automatically, on a number both sides already know from the design basis.

🏢

Scales across the portfolio

A Cx firm or owner running ten projects a year shouldn't renegotiate ten times. One org-level platform agreement covers unlimited users and every project; each new project just adds its MW at the locked rate. Expansion is automatic — the more they build, the more they pay, with no new contract.

The value metric

We price per commissioned megawatt — fixed in the design basis, knowable before day one

The megawatt is the unit data-center buyers already think and budget in. Crucially, a project's commissioned capacity is set in the design documents before commissioning starts — it doesn't drift with the schedule, it can't be inflated by failures, and it can't be gamed down. That's what makes per-MW both predictable (known up front) and fair (scales with value). It's the same shape the best AEC software already uses — Procore prices on construction value with unlimited users1 — but on a cleaner unit than the buyer's own revenue.

Annual platform fee + (commissioned MW × locked rate)
Both terms are fixed at signing. The buyer can do this arithmetic themselves — that's the point.

The rate card — locked for the contract term

Marginal bands, like a tax bracket: each MW is priced in its band, so totals always move smoothly — a 100 MW campus never costs less than a 99 MW one. The rate is fixed at signing for the whole project (or the multi-year term), so mid-build it cannot change.

×1.5 on the MW rate. High-density and liquid-cooled builds cost materially more to build (~$15–20M+/MW vs ~$11M) and to commission — so they carry proportionally more value and a higher rate.5

Per-project floor

Each project is billed as a minimum of 5 MW, so a tiny edge site still clears our onboarding cost — the same per-unit-minimum pattern used by per-asset and per-bed platforms.3

Always included, never metered

Unlimited users — every sub, tech, witness, and owner-side reviewer. Plus the lesson engine, the integrations, and the audit trail. Adding people never changes the bill.

Honest status: the structure — fixed platform fee + per-MW on nameplate capacity, unlimited users, rate locked at signing — is decided and grounded in how AEC and vertical SaaS actually price. The exact dollar figures above are validated hypotheses, not yet market-tested. No commissioning incumbent publishes a per-MW software rate, so we are defining this benchmark — the first design-partner conversations exist to pressure-test the numbers before we quote a real customer.

The discipline

What we will never put on the invoice

Each of these is a common SaaS pricing lever we deliberately reject — because each one either punishes the behavior we want, or bills the buyer for something outside their control. Rejecting them is what makes the number predictable.

✕
Per seat.

Charging per user taxes the one thing we most want: getting every subcontractor, technician, and witness into the system. It also makes the bill balloon as the team grows — unpredictable, and the opposite of our goal. Incumbents do this (CxPlanner ~$65/user/mo, BlueRithm ~$135/user/mo2); we commit to unlimited users, always.

✕
Metered usage.

Billing per test run, per deviation logged, per API call, or per document means the buyer can't know the total in advance — and it perversely rewards under-recording the very work we exist to capture. The consumption-metered model with a vendor-only minimum (e.g. Bentley E365) is exactly the anti-pattern we avoid.1

✕
Outcome / delay-based fees.

A share tied to on-time handover sounds aligned, but handover slips for reasons the buyer doesn't control — upstream design and construction delays land on commissioning. Pricing on that asks them to pay for someone else's slip. It also can't be budgeted. Powerful narrative, uninvoiceable — permanently off the table, not a "v2."

✕
Surprise overages.

The rate is locked at signing for the term. If a project grows, the new MW are priced at the same published rate the buyer already saw — there is no separate overage rate, no renegotiation, no blank check.

Why this beats the incumbent axis

They price like a document tool. We price like the value we de-risk.

📄

The incumbent trap

CxPlanner, BlueRithm, CxAlloy and Facility Grid price per seat or per project — a few thousand dollars against a multi-million-dollar Cx budget. It's predictable, but it doesn't scale with value and it caps their own ceiling. We will not fight on that axis; it's a race to a tiny number.

⚙️

Our move

Keep their one genuinely buyer-friendly idea — unlimited users, predictable bill — and swap their value metric for the megawatt. Same predictability the buyer wants, but the number now scales with the project and the portfolio. The authorities on usage pricing agree: pick a metric that's known up front, grows with customer value, and can't be gamed.4
