Where commissioning fits

The last gate between capital and revenue

Every facility goes through the same lifecycle. Commissioning is the final phase — the structured proof that what was designed and built actually works. It's a small slice of the budget and the schedule, but nothing earns a dollar until it's done: no handover, no occupancy permit, no tenants, no revenue.

Phase 1

Design

Engineers translate the owner's requirements into drawings, specifications, and sequences of operations — the "design intent."

Phase 2

Build

Contractors construct the facility and install thousands of pieces of equipment across mechanical, electrical, and controls disciplines.

Phase 3 — CXPro's home

Commission

Every system is tested against design intent, level by level (L1–L5), witnessed, signed, and documented — the legal and contractual gate to opening.

Phase 4

Operate

The owner runs the facility for decades — inheriting whatever quality, documentation, and knowledge the commissioning phase produced.

The structural problem: commissioning sits at the end of the schedule, so it inherits every upstream delay — and it gates 100% of the revenue. When earlier phases slip, commissioning gets compressed; when commissioning is compressed, quality risk and delay costs explode. That's why it's consistently reported as the #1 reason for handover failures in data center construction.1

Sources & how much to trust them

We grade our own numbers. Strong = peer-reviewed or national-laboratory research. Industry research = credible original research accessed via secondary summaries. Estimate / vendor-reported = directionally useful, but from a party with something to sell (including, on this page, us).

  1. Estimate / vendor-reported Data center delay rates (76% delayed, ~12% on time): The Fast Mode (expert opinion, 2025) and industry statistics roundups. Survey methodology not published — treat as directional, consistent with the broader finding that ~9 in 10 large infrastructure projects run over schedule. The same reporting identifies commissioning delays as a leading cause of handover failures.
  2. Estimate / vendor-reported ~$14.2M/month delay cost on a 60 MW facility: CMiC industry analysis. Derived from market lease rates and lost-revenue arithmetic, not audited project data — the order of magnitude is what matters.
  3. Industry research Rework at 5–10% of project cost; miscommunication driving ~26%: original research by the Construction Industry Institute and FMI/Autodesk, accessed via PlanRadar and OpenSpace summaries. The underlying CII research is a respected academic-industry consortium; the summarizing pages are vendors. Ranges across studies run 2–20%.
  4. Estimate / vendor-reported $30–150M exposure from a 3-month slip on a 50 MW campus: Exto analysis. A scenario model with stated assumptions (stranded capital + deferred revenue + liquidated damages), not measured outcomes. Useful as a framing of the exposure classes, not as a forecast.
  5. Strong 16% median energy savings, 4.5× benefit-cost, <2-year payback: Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, "Building commissioning costs and savings across three decades and 1,500 North American buildings" (peer-reviewed). A U.S. national laboratory studying the largest commissioning dataset ever assembled — the most defensible number on this page. Note: it measures existing-building commissioning; new-construction figures are more modest (1.1× benefit-cost, ~4.2-year payback).
  6. Estimate / vendor-reported Closeout taking 4× longer than planned: Buildots internal project data. A vendor's own analysis of its customers' projects — self-interested but consistent with practitioner experience of end-loaded punch lists.

How the team works together

Dedicated where you work. Shared where you coordinate.

Commissioning projects don't fail inside one company's work — they fail in the seams between companies and disciplines. CXPro is built on one simple split: every role gets a dedicated surface shaped around their own job, and all of it flows into one shared backbone the whole team trusts. The AI follows the same split.

🎯

Your dedicated view

Each role lands on a home built for their day — the engineer's test board, the CM's look-ahead of witnesses and crews, the tech's assignment list with one-tap pass/fail, the OCA's compliance posture. Your inbox holds only what you own; your permissions let you write your scope. No two roles see the same screen, because no two roles do the same job.

🤝

The shared backbone

Underneath every view: one asset registry, one test schedule, one deviation board, one document library, one audit log. Everyone reads from the same truth — so the status meeting stops being a reconciliation exercise. When a tech fails a step, the engineer, the CM, and the OCA all see the same fact, in their own context, at the same moment.

🧑‍💻

AI dedicated to you

Each role's AI agent is scoped to that role's job and permissions: the field assistant explains the step in front of you; the engineer's agent drafts and triages within their discipline; the CM's agent understands where the project hurts — crews, sequencing, slips — and proposes the re-plan when reality changes. And the agents coordinate with each other: one disruption becomes a few well-framed decisions, each routed to the person whose call it is. See the choreography →

🌐

AI for the whole team

All agents draw on the same shared substrate — one indexed document library, one citation standard, one lesson engine. When an engineer corrects an AI draft, every role's future answers get better. Dedicated agents, shared intelligence: the team compounds, not just the individual.

Why CXPro is different

Three principles behind the product

⚡

Action-centric, not data-centric

CXPro isn't a database with forms. Every record — a test, a deviation, an RFI — has a lifecycle, and the interface always shows you the next allowed action for your role. Less hunting, more doing.

🤖

AI drafts, humans decide

AI is a first-class team member that prepares work — procedures, triage recommendations, summaries — but it can never change project state on its own. A human confirms every AI proposal, and that confirmation is logged.

🧾

Audit-ready by construction

Every state transition, by every actor — human or AI — lands in a tamper-evident audit log. Handover dossiers and regulator conversations stop being archaeology projects.
