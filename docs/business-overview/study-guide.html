What commissioning is — and the documents that carry it

Commissioning (Cx) is a quality-assurance process that verifies and documents that a facility's systems are planned, designed, installed, tested, operated, and maintained to meet the Owner's Project Requirements. It is not a single inspection at the end — it runs the whole project lifecycle. Every exam tests these core documents; learn them cold.

DocumentWhat it isWho owns it
OPR — Owner's Project RequirementsThe written statement of the owner's functional needs, expectations, and success criteria. The "north star" every later decision is measured against. Created early, updated throughout.Owner (often facilitated by the CxA)
BoD — Basis of DesignThe design team's documented response to the OPR — concepts, assumptions, standards, and calculations showing how the design meets the OPR.Design team (A/E)
Commissioning PlanThe roadmap: scope, team and roles, schedule, communication protocols, and the Cx processes/forms to be used. A living document.Commissioning Authority (CxA)
Commissioning Specifications (Division 01 91 00)The contractual Cx requirements written into the construction documents — what contractors must do, witness, and document.CxA / design team
Checklists (construction / installation / prefunctional)Component-level verification that equipment is delivered, installed, and ready — completed by the installing contractor, spot-verified by the CxA.Contractors → verified by CxA
Functional Performance Test (FPT) proceduresScripted, step-by-step tests proving each system performs through its sequences of operation under all modes and failure conditions.CxA writes, team executes
Issues / Deviation LogThe running record of every deficiency found, its severity, owner, and resolution. The backbone of accountability.CxA
Systems ManualThe operator's handover package: OPR/BoD, sequences, as-builts, O&M data, test records, and training — what runs the building for the next 20 years.CxA compiles
Commissioning ReportThe final deliverable: what was tested, what was found, what was resolved, and what remains (deferred/seasonal). The audit trail.CxA

Exam hook: if you can recite the chain OPR → BoD → Cx Plan → Cx Specs → Checklists → FPTs → Issues Log → Systems Manual → Cx Report and say who owns each and when it's created, you've answered a large share of every exam. This is also exactly the CXPro data model — which is why earning these certs and building the product reinforce each other.

The team and the four project phases

ASHRAE Guideline 0 organizes commissioning across the project lifecycle. Learn what the CxA does in each phase — the exams are heavily phase-structured.

🧑‍🔧

The commissioning team

The Commissioning Authority (CxA / CxP) leads the process and is ideally independent (a hard requirement for the ACG CxA credential). Around them: the Owner (sets the OPR), the design team (A/E, owns the BoD), the construction manager / GC, the trade subcontractors (mechanical, electrical, controls), the TAB contractor, equipment vendors, and the facility / O&M staff who inherit the building.

🎯

Why independence matters

The CxA verifies the work of the parties they coordinate. Independence (reporting to the owner, not the GC) is what makes that verification credible — and it's a defining, annually-re-verified condition of the ACG CxA credential. Other credentials (CCP, BCxP) allow owner's-staff or contractor CxAs but still stress documented objectivity.

Phase 1 — Pre-Design / Planning

Develop the OPR, select the CxA early, define commissioning scope and budget, and draft the initial Commissioning Plan. The single highest-leverage phase: catching requirement gaps here is free; catching them in the field is expensive.

Phase 2 — Design

The CxA performs a commissioning design review (does the design satisfy the OPR?), reviews the BoD, writes the Cx specifications into the construction documents, updates the Cx Plan, and outlines the functional tests to come.

Phase 3 — Construction

The heart of execution: submittal review, installation / prefunctional checklists, equipment start-up, functional performance testing, the issues log, operator training, and assembling the Systems Manual.

Phase 4 — Occupancy & Operations

Deferred and seasonal testing (e.g. test heating in winter, cooling in summer), a near-warranty-end review (~10 months in), ongoing commissioning, and capturing lessons. Commissioning doesn't end at handover.

The testing levels — L1 through L5

Building commissioning verifies in escalating steps: component → system → whole-facility. The mission-critical / data-center world formalizes these as Levels 1–5 — the exact framing CXPro is built around, and what data-center buyers expect.

Level 1

Factory Witness

Witness critical equipment tested at the factory before it ships (FWT/FAT) — generators, switchgear, UPS, chillers. Catch defects before they're on site.

Level 2

Pre-Functional / Installation

Verify each component arrived and was installed correctly and completely — the prefunctional checklists. Static, component-level readiness.

Level 3

Start-up / Energization

Manufacturer start-up, point-to-point controls checkout, and first safe operation of each piece of equipment.

Level 4

Functional Performance

Each system is run through its full sequences of operation — every mode, setpoint, alarm, and failure condition — individually. Witnessed and documented.

Level 5

Integrated Systems Test

All systems tested together under simulated failure scenarios — e.g. loss of utility power → generators start, UPS carries the load, cooling rides through. The ultimate proof for a data center.

The conceptual core (works for any building): prefunctional (is it installed right?) → functional (does the system do what its sequence says?) → integrated (do all systems behave correctly together, including under failure?). Functional Performance Testing is where commissioning earns its name: a scripted test that drives a system through every operating and failure mode and documents pass/fail against the OPR and the sequence of operation.

Sequences of operation, controls & test & balance

You can't write or judge a functional test without understanding the control logic and airflow/water balance underneath it. This is the technical substrate the exams assume — and where the free controls resources earn their keep.

🔁

Sequences of operation

The written control logic the building automation system (BAS) executes — when a fan starts, how a valve modulates, what happens on an alarm. ASHRAE Guideline 36 standardizes high-performance sequences; study the free LBNL OpenBuildingControl implementation. Functional tests exist to prove the installed sequence matches the design.

🎚️

BAS / DDC controls

Direct digital control: sensors, controllers, actuators, points lists, and point-to-point checkout. Learn the basics free from Smart Buildings Academy, the Taylor BAS Best Practices Guide, and Belimo University.

💨

Test & Balance (TAB)

Measuring and adjusting air and water flows to design values — a prerequisite to functional testing (you can't test performance on an unbalanced system). Free coverage in FEMP O&M Guide Ch. 9 and UFGS 23 05 93.

Existing buildings, ongoing Cx & the mission-critical specifics

♻️

Retro- & ongoing commissioning

Retrocommissioning (RCx) applies the process to an existing building to find and fix operational drift — typically the highest-ROI energy work available (LBNL: low cost, fast payback). Monitoring-based Cx (MBCx) makes it continuous, using trend data, analytics, and fault detection & diagnostics (FDD). Learn it free via PNNL Building Re-tuning and FEMP femp01. Existing-building Cx is a large, separately-counted section on the CCP and BCxP exams.

🏭

Data-center / mission-critical Cx

The systems that make L5 integrated testing matter: generators, UPS, switchgear, ATS, and cooling (CRAC/CRAH, chilled water, increasingly liquid cooling). The defining test is failure simulation — loss of power, loss of cooling — against Uptime Institute Tier concepts (concurrent maintainability, fault tolerance). Free reading: Uptime Institute Journal and NETA World (electrical testing).

Note on scope: no commissioning certification is data-center-specific — the credentials test the general building Cx process, and you apply it to data centers. So master the process first (sections 1–4 above), then layer the mission-critical specifics for the actual job. This is also CXPro's wedge: we encode the data-center L1–L5 workflow the generic certs don't teach.

A free 8-week study plan

Weeks 1–2

Map the territory

blueprints + definitions

Read the BCxP Guidebook (JTA) and CCP Handbook cover to cover; build a flashcard deck from the BCxA Definitions. Goal: know what's tested and speak the vocabulary.

Weeks 3–4

Learn the process

Std 202 + PNNL + GSA

Read Standard 202 (free read-only), the PNNL Guide, and the GSA Guide. Write out the OPR→Cx Report document chain and the four-phase activities from memory.

Weeks 5–6

Get technical

controls, sequences, FPT

Work through LBNL OpenBuildingControl sequences and the Smart Buildings Academy / MEP Academy basics; study the ACG Appendix D functional test scripts line by line. Do PNNL Re-tuning + FEMP femp01/femp66.

Weeks 7–8

Drill & verticalize

practice + mission-critical

Take the free BCxP practice exam, re-test weak content areas, then layer the data-center specifics (Uptime Tiers, generator/UPS/IST) from the Uptime and NETA journals. Buy Guideline 0 if you want the one authoritative reference before sitting.
