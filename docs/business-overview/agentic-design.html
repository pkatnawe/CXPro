The agentic system: a living model of your project — and the research behind it.

CXPro isn't forms with a chatbot. It's a continuously-maintained model of a commissioning project: standardized states seeded by a strong AI inception baseline, kept aligned to a global objective by per-role agents, mindful of the real team and the real-world delays, and improving from lightweight feedback. This page is the architecture and the published research it rests on. Full internal write-up: docs/agentic-design-foundations.md. Strictly internal.

What CXPro actually is

Not forms with AI — a living, self-improving model of the project

The lean-in for a hyperscaler is everyday proven value through a simple interface; the moat is the agentic engine plus the feedback that compounds. Four pillars hold it up.

🧩

Standardized project states

A canonical, auditable model of the commissioning program — instantiated automatically from the design package at inception, then kept current as reality changes. Comparable across projects, which is what makes the lesson loop and the benchmark possible.

🎯

A global project objective

One objective that balances the project's requirements, feasibility, schedule risk, and human/safety constraints — decomposed into a DAG of verifiable sub-goals. It's also the verifier: the rubric that ranks "what's the next best action / what's at risk to handover."

🧑‍🔧

Per-role agents, recursive subgoals

One agent per team member (OCA, Cx Engineer, CM, Field Tech, Design Engineer, Owner/FM), each pursuing its own subgoals in service of the global objective — orchestrator-worker, kept aligned, never drifting from the whole.

🫶

Mindful of humans & reality

Aware of who's on the team, their responsibilities and availability, and the realities of construction and commissioning slips — so it plans around delays deliberately, not by hoping nothing goes wrong. AI drafts; a human always decides.

How it runs

The loop: inception → live → feedback → optimize → measure

Usable day one from the documents the customer already hands over; better with every answer. Onboarding is the product.

Inception — a strong AI baseline

Ingest the design package; sample many candidate project states/plans and select the best with the global-objective judge (test-time scaling: sample → verify → select). The standardized states are instantiated, not hand-built.

Live — orchestrated, cited, governed

The orchestrator runs the global objective; per-role agents draft and route. Every claim is cited; the agent refuses when unsure; risky/state-changing actions pass a human-in-the-loop gate. Same harness as inception and eval.

Feedback — the "mini grill-me"

Lightweight, role-scoped multi-select prompts dropped at the right moments. They simultaneously collect expert feedback, refine the state, and onboard the team — and they're the labels that calibrate the judge.

Optimize — reflective, not RL

The aligned judge drives automated improvement of each role agent's prompts and skills (reflective prompt evolution), versioned and gated. No RL loop, no manual prompt-tuning.

Measure — CxBench gates releases

A multi-regime, deliberately hard, multimodal benchmark is the held-out judge. No regression ships. The same artifacts that make the product good are the research contribution.

The flywheel: every project's mini-grill-me feedback sharpens the judge and the agents → the inception baseline gets stronger → the moat compounds. That's the lesson loop — now with a name and a method.

The load-bearing piece

The objective that ranks plans is the whole game

Every result above — test-time scaling, reflective optimization, the bridge, the drift monitor — bottlenecks on one thing: a judge that tracks expert quality. Get it right and compute trades up to quality. Get it wrong and you reward-hack: optimization makes the agent worse while the score rises. So we build the judge first.

🧮

Decompose into critics

Not one monolith: feasibility, dependency ordering (L1→L5 gating), requirement coverage (every OPR / sequence item tested — a deterministic match, not "LLM vibes"), and risk/safety. Keep a deterministic "sound floor" a persuasive-but-wrong plan can't argue away.

🧠

Calibrate from few labels

Dual memory (MemAlign): generalizable principles + episodic edge cases; ~2–10 expert examples per critic, improving as feedback accumulates. This is exactly what the mini-grill-me collects. Gate on judge↔expert agreement (Cohen's κ); alarm on drift.

🛡️

Guard against reward hacking

Over-optimization is law-like — it hits even best-of-N with no training. Cap optimization pressure, ensemble diverse critics, and never optimize against an uncalibrated judge. The judge is the moat and the liability.

Why this matters for commissioning specifically: auto-generated rubrics collapse on niche, factual domains — and commissioning is exactly that. Human episodic anchors (the certified-expert feedback we collect) are non-negotiable. This is also why earning the certifications and authoring the knowledge base ourselves directly sharpens the judge.

Priority order

We already have the hard parts — here's the build

The backend already has a LangGraph agent, pgvector retrieval, cited + refuse-on-low-confidence, and DSPy. The missing pieces, in dependency order:

1

An aligned judge per role

everything depends on this

Calibrated from mini-grill-me feedback (MemAlign-style: semantic + episodic memory), decomposed into feasibility / ordering / coverage / risk critics with a deterministic sound floor.

2

CxBench as the held-out judge

measure & gate

Multi-regime, hard, multimodal. We have the seed (a 56-item Cx-Engineer set + a document-intelligence track design); expand it.

3

The reflective optimization loop

GEPA / optimize_anything

Refine each role agent's prompts + skills against the aligned judge; versioned and gated.

4

Test-time sample-and-select

the inception baseline

Generate N candidate project states/plans → verify with the global-objective judge → keep the best.

5

The global-objective layer

GDE / RRM / SIL-style

Formalize the verifiable-subgoal DAG and the live drift monitor — the rubric that balances requirements, feasibility, risk, and human/safety. This rubric is the verifier.

6

Meta-harness (later, earned)

once the judge is trusted

ADAS/DGM-style optimization of the agent topology itself — a strong long-term research story, safe only after the judge is trustworthy.

The throughline: the same artifacts that make the product good — an aligned judge, optimized role agents, CxBench — are the research contribution. That's the product-and-research balance the project is built to hit. See the grounding plan on Agent Training and the benchmark in corpus/eval/.
