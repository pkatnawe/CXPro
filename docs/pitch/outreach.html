How we'd reach customers

Who we'd sell to

Our plan for getting the first customers — and the open questions we're still working through.

Who buys

A large, growing pool of buyers — and two ways in

Every data center being built needs commissioning, and third-party commissioning is increasingly required, so the buyer pool is large and growing (a ~$2B market today, projected to roughly double by 2033). There are two good front doors, and we can knock on both.

🏢

The companies building the data centers

AI clouds like FluidStack, Crusoe, and CoreWeave, plus the big hyperscalers. They have the budget and the biggest pipelines, but they're harder to get into and slower to buy.

🛠️

The commissioning firms easiest start

Independent firms that do the commissioning for everyone. Easier to reach, and once they adopt it they bring it to every project they win.

One thing we've learned matters: to win a deal we need both the budget-holder and the person who'll actually use it bought in. An excited exec who isn't the user can't make a team adopt anything.

The plan

Start with a few hands-on design partners

Land 5–10 design partners through warm intros

Warm introductions work far better than cold outreach. The goal is a handful of real customers using it on live projects.

Make them paid, not free

A real (discounted) contract, in exchange for close access and weekly time with us. Paying customers take it seriously; free pilots don't tell us if it's worth paying for.

Help the experts, don't replace them

This is a relationship-driven industry. We make the commissioning expert faster and better — we never try to cut them out.

Time it to their hiring

When a company is hiring commissioning staff, it means a build is close to done — the perfect moment to reach out. FluidStack is hiring for it right now.

Open questions

What we're still working through

Getting to customers

  • First wedge: go straight to the data-center builders, or start with the commissioning firms that already hold the relationships?
  • Approaching a builder like FluidStack: who to reach, what opens the door, and how to move fast without a year-long procurement cycle.
  • The trust & security bar: how an AI product gets past security review and procurement at a large, cautious buyer.
  • The Canadian / sovereign-AI market: a wave of sovereign data centers is being built in Canada (Bell, Telus, federal compute) that all need commissioning — is data residency a real wedge, and how would we capture it?
  • Founder-led vs. hiring: how long the founders should run sales before bringing in an industry seller.
